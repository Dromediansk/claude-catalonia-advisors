# General safety for tourists in Barcelona

Barcelona is broadly safe for visitors. Violent crime against tourists is rare. The dominant threat is **petty theft** (pickpocketing, bag snatching, phone grabs). Pickpocketing accounts for roughly half of all reported crime in the city, with around 250+ thefts reported per day.

## Emergency numbers

- **112** — pan-European emergency number (police, ambulance, fire). English-speaking operators available.
- **091** — Policia Nacional direct.
- **092** — Guàrdia Urbana (local Barcelona police) direct.
- **088** — Mossos d'Esquadra (Catalan regional police) direct.
- For lost/stolen items, file a *denúncia* (police report) — needed for insurance claims. Can be done online via Mossos website or in person at any commissaria.

## Pickpocket hotspots

Stay especially alert in:

- **La Rambla (Las Ramblas)** — the entire stretch from Plaça de Catalunya to the Mirador de Colom. Worst around the **Mercat de la Boqueria** (where tourists stop to photograph) and around street performers.
- **Metro L3 (green line)** — connects Sagrada Família, Passeig de Gràcia, Catalunya, Liceu, Drassanes. The single most-targeted line. L1 (red) and L4 (yellow) are also high-risk through the centre.
- **Sagrada Família** — the queue, the Metro exits (Sagrada Família L2/L5 station), and the gardens opposite.
- **Plaça de Catalunya** — the convergence point of L1, L3, FGC, Rodalies and the airport bus.
- **Barri Gòtic (Gothic Quarter)** — narrow streets, especially after dark. Watch Plaça Reial, Carrer dels Escudellers, Carrer de la Boqueria.
- **Park Güell** entrances and Bunkers del Carmel viewpoint at sunset.
- **Plaça d'Espanya / Montjuïc magic fountain** crowds.
- **Estació de Sants** (Barcelona's main train station) — luggage targeted.
- **Beaches** — Platja de la Barceloneta, Platja de Sant Sebastià. Towel-lift thefts while you swim.

## Tactics pickpockets use

- Teams of 2–4 working in coordination. One distracts, one lifts, one runs.
- Crowding you at metro doors as they close — the lifter steps off as doors shut.
- "Helpful" strangers pointing at a map, asking the time, offering directions.
- Bumping into you in a crowded bar or on the Rambla.
- Drape a jacket or newspaper over your bag/phone on the café table, lift the phone underneath.

## Defensive habits

- Front pockets only, or zipped inside pocket. Never a back pocket.
- Wear backpack on your **chest** in dense crowds and on the metro.
- Never leave a phone face-up on a café table near the street (terrace seating on La Rambla is the worst spot).
- Keep one hand on your bag when entering/exiting the metro.
- Don't put your bag on the back of your chair or on the floor between your feet — wrap the strap around your leg or use a chair hook.
- At the beach: bring only what you can carry into the water in a waterproof pouch.
- Split cash + cards across two places.

## Neighbourhoods that warrant caution after dark

- **El Raval (southern half)** — south of Carrer del Carme, around **Carrer d'En Robador** and the streets behind the MACBA. Visible drug activity, sex work, and opportunistic theft after midnight. The northern half (around MACBA, CCCB, Rambla del Raval) is fine. Stick to well-lit streets like **Carrer Joaquín Costa**.
- **Barceloneta beachfront** — Passeig Marítim and Platja de la Barceloneta late at night. Phone/bag snatches from passing scooters and bikes spike after sunset. The neighbourhood itself is residential and fine; the **beach promenade after midnight** is the risk.
- **La Rambla after midnight** — quieter and the remaining crowd skews toward drunk tourists and the people who prey on them.
- **Plaça George Orwell ("Plaça del Trippy")** in Barri Gòtic late at night.
- **Plaça dels Àngels** (MACBA skater square) — fine by day, drug dealing after dark.

None of these are no-go zones — just be sober, walk with purpose, avoid empty side streets, and don't flash phones/jewellery.

## The three police forces (and what each handles)

Spain has overlapping jurisdictions. In Barcelona you may encounter three uniformed forces:

- **Mossos d'Esquadra** — Catalan regional police. **Blue uniforms with red trim**, red beret. Primary force for serious crime in Catalonia: theft, assault, investigations, traffic outside Barcelona city, public order. **This is who you file most reports with.** Main tourist commissaria: **Carrer Nou de la Rambla, 76–80** (open 24/7, English-speaking officers, dedicated tourist desk).
- **Guàrdia Urbana** — Barcelona municipal police. **Navy blue uniforms**, checkered band on cap. Local urban policing inside the city of Barcelona only: traffic, parking, minor incidents, neighbourhood patrols, ordinance enforcement. Often the first responders on La Rambla.
- **Policía Nacional** — Spanish national police. **Dark blue uniforms**. Handles immigration, passports/DNI, terrorism, organised crime, border control. **This is who you go to for passport theft/replacement** (the foreigners' office is at **Passeig de Sant Joan, 189**).
- **Guardia Civil** — green uniforms. Rural areas, highways, airports, ports. You may see them at El Prat or on the AP-7 motorway, rarely in central Barcelona.

For pickpocket reports as a tourist: Mossos at Nou de la Rambla is the right place. For a stolen passport: Policía Nacional. For a traffic incident inside the city: Guàrdia Urbana.

## General health & safety

- Tap water is **safe to drink** throughout Barcelona, though it tastes mineral-heavy; locals often buy bottled.
- Pharmacies (**farmàcies**, green cross) are plentiful; at least one in each neighbourhood is open 24h on rotation — list posted on every pharmacy door.
- EU citizens: bring your EHIC/GHIC card. Non-EU: travel insurance strongly recommended.
- Summer heat: 35°C+ days are common in July–August. Carry water, use sunscreen, avoid 13:00–17:00 sun exposure.
