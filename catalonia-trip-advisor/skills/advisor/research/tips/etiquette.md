# Etiquette in Barcelona and Catalonia

Catalans are direct, polite, and pragmatic. You won't accidentally cause offence by being yourself, but a few specific habits mark you as someone who's paid attention.

## Language: Catalan vs Spanish

- **Catalonia is bilingual.** Catalan (català) is the primary local language; Spanish (castellà / castellano) is also official. Almost everyone speaks both.
- Street signs, menus, and official communication are in Catalan first.
- **Trying a few Catalan phrases is appreciated**, even more than Spanish in Barcelona. "Bon dia", "gràcies", "si us plau", "adéu" go a long way.
- **Never assume Catalans are "Spanish first"** — for many it's a sensitive identity point. Don't conflate Catalonia and Spain in conversation.
- English is widely understood in the tourist core and by anyone under 40. Less so in older bars, taxis, and outside the centre.

## Greetings

- **Two cheek kisses (*dos petons*)** — social, between friends or when being introduced through a friend. Start with the **left** cheek (your right side touches first). It's air-kiss-with-cheek-contact, not lip-on-cheek.
- **Pattern:**
  - Woman ↔ woman: two kisses.
  - Woman ↔ man: two kisses.
  - Man ↔ man: handshake (unless close friends/family — then hug or kisses).
- **Professional / business:** handshake. Firm, brief, with eye contact. Kisses if the relationship warms up later.
- **First meeting in a casual social setting:** kisses are common even on introduction. Follow the local's lead — they will initiate.
- **Greeting groups:** if entering a small gathering, greet each person individually (kiss or handshake) rather than a generic "hi to everyone".
- **Phrases through the day:**
  - **Bon dia** — good morning / good day (until ~14:00)
  - **Bona tarda** — good afternoon (~14:00 to dusk)
  - **Bona nit** — good night / good evening (after dark; both greeting and farewell)
  - **Adéu / Fins després** — goodbye / see you later
  - Spanish equivalents: *buenos días, buenas tardes, buenas noches, adiós*.

## Restaurant & bar etiquette

- **Wait to be seated** in sit-down restaurants. At a bar/tapas counter, claim a spot yourself.
- **Service is unrushed by design.** The bill won't appear unless you ask — staff consider bringing it early to be rude (rushing you out).
- **Asking for the bill:**
  - Catalan: **"El compte, si us plau"** (el COMP-tuh, see oos PLAH-oo)
  - Spanish: **"La cuenta, por favor"**
  - Universal: a small written-in-the-air gesture works fine.
- **Splitting bills is completely normal.** Say **"podem pagar per separat?"** (can we pay separately?) or just hand over multiple cards. Strict 50/50 splits, item-by-item splits — all fine.
- **Bread (pa)** often appears unrequested. If it's free, the bill won't list it. If there's a small *cobert* (cover charge), you're paying for it — fine to refuse the basket if you don't want to pay.
- **Table manners:** both hands on/above the table (not in your lap). Don't start eating until everyone is served — wait for "**bon profit**" / "**que aproveche**" (≈ enjoy your meal).
- **Don't ask to substitute** half the dish or make heavy modifications. Spanish/Catalan kitchens cook what's on the menu; allergies and vegetarian/vegan needs are respected, but "can I have the paella without seafood" is met with confusion.
- **Sangria** is a tourist drink. Locals drink *vermut*, *cava*, beer (*canya*), or wine. No judgement, just heads up.
- **Eating tapas:** small plates shared in the middle. Pace yourself — order 2–3 per person, decide as you go, no need to order everything at once.

## Dress code

### Sagrada Família and other churches

Strictly enforced:

- **Shoulders covered** — no spaghetti straps, vests, strapless tops, very deep necklines.
- **Skirts / shorts must reach the knee** (or close to it — at minimum mid-thigh, but knee-length is the safe rule).
- **No flip-flops, no swimwear, no see-through clothing.**
- **No hats / caps / hoods** inside the basilica (religious/medical exceptions).
- **Bare feet not allowed.**
- A light scarf or thin overshirt in your day bag solves this in summer.

The same rules apply at the **Catedral de Barcelona**, **Santa Maria del Mar**, **Monestir de Pedralbes**, **Montserrat**, and most active churches.

### Beach vs city

- **Swimwear is for the beach only.** Walking through Barceloneta in a bikini or shirtless beyond the boardwalk is **fined by Guàrdia Urbana** (€100–€300 [verify]). Cover up before crossing Passeig de Joan de Borbó.
- **No bare chests in the city** — supermarkets and bars will refuse service.
- **Locals dress neatly** for the city. Smart casual is the default — clean trainers, a shirt or nice top, no athletic gear off the gym/run.
- **Nightlife / nicer restaurants:** smart casual. Beach clubs may have dress codes (no flip-flops, no shorts for men) — check.

## Quiet hours in apartments

- **Quiet hours (descans dels veïns):** typically **22:00 / 23:00 – 08:00** weekdays and Sundays. Slightly later on Friday/Saturday nights — generally **00:00 – 09:00**.
- Catalan buildings have thin walls and stone floors. Heels, dragged furniture, loud music, and shouted conversations all carry.
- **Tourist apartments are heavily regulated.** Many neighbours actively report parties — fines for the host can be steep, and you can be asked to leave.
- **Tancar la porta amb suavitat** — close the building's heavy main door quietly, especially late at night. Slamming it echoes through the entire stairwell.
- Don't bring beach-trip groups back at 02:00 making noise on the street outside the building.

## Smoking, drinking, public space

- **Smoking** banned in indoor public spaces (bars, restaurants, clubs). On terraces it depends on the establishment.
- **Drinking alcohol in the street** is technically prohibited and fines exist [verify], though enforcement is patchy outside specific zones (around La Rambla, beachfront).
- **Beach drinking:** also officially restricted. Glass on the beach is forbidden.
- **Cannabis:** private clubs exist (members only, not for tourists). Buying or smoking in public is illegal and fined.
- **Public urination, topless cycling, etc.:** fines under Barcelona's *Ordenança de Civisme*, regularly enforced near La Rambla and Barceloneta.

## Small social things that matter

- **Say hello when entering small shops and bars** — "Bon dia" or "Hola" — and "Adéu" / "Gràcies" on the way out. Silent entry is rude.
- **Personal space** is closer than in northern Europe; queues are smaller, people stand nearer in conversation.
- **Punctuality:** business meetings on time. Social plans: 15 minutes late is fine, an hour late is normal for casual hangouts.
- **Photographing people:** ask. Especially elderly people, children, market vendors. The Boqueria has signs asking photographers to buy something or move on.
- **Don't talk loudly in restaurants and metros** — Catalans are not as loud as the southern Spanish stereotype suggests. Volume calibrates to the room.
- **Political topics** (Catalan independence, the *procés*) — fine to ask, polarising. Don't take strong positions until you've heard the other person.
- **Don't call Catalans "Spanish"** as if it's a fixed fact. "Are you from Spain?" → "I'm from Catalonia" is a common, telling reply.
- **Tipping:** see `tipping.md`. Short version: not US-style, just round up.
