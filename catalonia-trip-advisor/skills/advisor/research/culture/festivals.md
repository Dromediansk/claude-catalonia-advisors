# Festivals and Annual Events

Catalonia has one of the densest festival calendars in Europe. The big ones below are the ones tourists most often ask about. Dates are 2026 unless noted; verify before travel.

## La Mercè (around 24 September)

- Barcelona's **biggest annual festival**, honoring the city's patron saint Mare de Déu de la Mercè.
- **2026 dates: 23–27 September** (official day always 24 September). [verify against official program when released]
- What happens:
  - ***Castells*** (human towers) in Plaça de Sant Jaume.
  - ***Correfoc*** (fire-run) — devils and dragons run through the streets spraying sparks. Wear long sleeves, a hat, closed shoes; cover your camera lens.
  - ***Gegants i capgrossos*** (giants and big-heads) parades.
  - ***Piromusical*** — closing fireworks-and-music show, usually at Montjuïc fountains.
  - Free concerts on multiple stages across the city.
- Mostly free, mostly outdoors, **packed**. Book accommodation early.

## Sant Jordi (23 April) — books and roses

- **Catalonia's Valentine's Day**, but cultural. Tradition: men give women a rose, women give men a book. Modernized — everyone gives everyone both.
- The whole of Barcelona becomes a giant outdoor book-and-flower fair. Authors sign in the streets. Best spots: La Rambla, Passeig de Gràcia, Rambla de Catalunya.
- Working day; not a public holiday in 2026 (a Thursday). [verify]
- The **Palau de la Generalitat opens to the public** — rare chance to see inside.
- It's also UNESCO World Book Day.

## Sant Joan (Nit de Sant Joan, night of 23 June)

- The shortest night of the year. **All-night party** with bonfires (*fogueres*) on the beach, fireworks and firecrackers everywhere, ***coca de Sant Joan*** (sweet bread with candied fruit) and cava.
- **24 June is a public holiday** in Catalonia.
- Survival tips:
  - The city sounds like a war zone — locals set off firecrackers from 20:00 onwards.
  - Beaches (Barceloneta, Bogatell, Mar Bella) are packed; arrive early; expect crowds, smoke, broken glass.
  - Public transport runs all night.
  - Not a quiet-sleep night anywhere central.

## Festa Major de Gràcia / Festes de Gràcia (mid-August)

- **2026 dates: 15–21 August**; inaugural proclamation 14 August.
- The Gràcia neighborhood **decorates ~20+ streets** with elaborate themed installations made of recycled materials. Neighbour committees spend months on them; there's a prize for the best.
- Free, all-day-and-night, with concerts, *correfocs*, *castells*, *sardanes*, *ball de bastons* (stick dance), and a designated "silent night" to give residents a break.
- Best visited in the evening when streets are lit. Tuesday is usually quieter than the weekend.

## Diada Nacional de Catalunya (11 September)

- Catalan **national day** — commemorates the fall of Barcelona in 1714.
- Expect mass political demonstrations organized by independence groups, official ceremonies, *senyeres* and *esteladas* on balconies.
- Many businesses close. Public transport runs; central streets may be closed.
- See `catalan-identity.md` for context.

## Reis / Three Kings / Cabalgata de Reyes (5–6 January)

- **The big gift-giving holiday in Spain**, larger than Christmas Day for children's presents.
- ***Cabalgata dels Reis*** (Three Kings Parade) — **evening of 5 January**, the kings arrive (in Barcelona, by boat at Port Vell around 17:00), then parade through the city throwing sweets to children.
- **6 January is a public holiday**; almost everything is closed.
- Traditional food: ***tortell de Reis***, a ring-shaped pastry with a hidden figurine and a bean — finding the figurine = wear the crown; finding the bean = pay for next year's *tortell*.

## Castells (human towers)

- **UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage** since 2010. A Catalan tradition, not just Barcelona.
- Teams (*colles*) build towers up to 10 levels high; a child (*enxaneta*) climbs to the top.
- **Where to see in Barcelona**:
  - **Plaça de Sant Jaume** during La Mercè (September) and Santa Eulàlia (February) — premier events.
  - Local neighborhood festivals (Festa Major de Gràcia, Sants, Sant Andreu, etc.).
  - Occasional Sunday performances; check the [Castellers de Barcelona](https://www.castellersdebarcelona.cat/) calendar.
- **Best castells outside Barcelona**: **Vilafranca del Penedès** (Concurs de Castells, biennial — next major event [verify 2026 schedule]) and **Tarragona**.

## Sardana (Catalan circle dance)

- Slow, hand-holding circle dance accompanied by a *cobla* (11-piece wind band). National dance of Catalonia.
- **Regular Barcelona spots**:
  - **In front of the Cathedral** (Pla de la Seu / Plaça Nova) — **Sundays around 11:15–12:00** (except August). Saturdays at 18:00 also sometimes. [verify current weekly schedule]
  - **Plaça de Sant Jaume** — occasional Sunday evenings.
- Free, no performers vs audience — anyone can join the circle. Place your bag in the middle to join.

## Other dates worth knowing

- **Santa Eulàlia (12 February)** — Barcelona's "winter festival", patron saint of the city. *Castells*, parades, family-oriented.
- **Carnaval** (Feb/Mar) — modest in Barcelona; bigger in Sitges (one of Spain's biggest, very LGBTQ-friendly).
- **Setmana Santa** (Holy Week, late March / April) — quieter in Catalonia than in Andalusia; some processions.
- **Primavera Sound** (late May / early June) — major international music festival at Parc del Fòrum.
- **Sónar** (mid-June) — electronic music and digital arts festival.
- **Festa Major de Sants** (late August) — like Gràcia's but in Sants neighborhood; less famous, easier to enjoy.
- **Festival Grec** (July) — performing arts festival at the Teatre Grec on Montjuïc.
