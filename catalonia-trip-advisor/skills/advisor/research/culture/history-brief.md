# Brief History of Catalonia

A traveler's skim — enough context to read a museum label without a textbook.

## Two-paragraph version

Catalonia's documented history begins as the Roman province around **Tarraco** (modern Tarragona), capital of *Hispania Citerior* — the Roman walls, amphitheater, and aqueduct are UNESCO-listed. After Visigothic and brief Moorish rule, the **Counts of Barcelona** (9th century onwards) became powerful enough to merge with Aragon in 1137, creating the **Crown of Aragon** — a medieval Mediterranean empire stretching to Valencia, the Balearics, Sicily, Sardinia, and briefly Athens. This is the period when Catalan institutions emerged: the *Generalitat*, the *Consell de Cent* in Barcelona, and the first written constitutions in Europe. The crowns of Aragon and Castile united under the *Reis Catòlics* (Ferdinand and Isabella) in 1469, but Catalonia kept its own laws and parliament for two more centuries.

The break came in **1714**: at the end of the War of the Spanish Succession, Barcelona fell to the Bourbons on 11 September, and the new king Philip V abolished Catalan institutions and banned the language in official use (the *Decrets de Nova Planta*). The 19th-century ***Renaixença*** revived Catalan language and literature, and fueled ***Modernisme*** — the cultural and architectural movement of Gaudí, Domènech i Montaner, and Puig i Cadafalch. Catalonia briefly regained autonomy in the **Second Republic (1931)**, lost it after the **Spanish Civil War (1936–39)**, and spent the **Franco dictatorship (1939–75)** with Catalan banned from public life. After Franco's death the **1978 Spanish Constitution** restored autonomy; the **2006 Statute** expanded it; a Constitutional Court ruling against parts of that Statute in 2010 helped trigger the modern independence movement, which peaked with the **2017 referendum and declaration of independence** (see `catalan-identity.md`).

## Quick reference timeline

- **218 BC–5th c.** — Roman *Tarraco*. Visit: Tarragona's amphitheater and walls.
- **9th c.–1137** — Counts of Barcelona. Wilfred the Hairy (*Guifré el Pilós*) traditionally credited with the four red stripes of the *senyera*.
- **1137–1469** — **Crown of Aragon**. Mediterranean trading power. Catalan is the language of administration and chronicles.
- **1469** — Dynastic union with Castile. Catalonia keeps its own institutions.
- **11 September 1714** — Barcelona falls; Catalan institutions abolished. Commemorated at the **Fossar de les Moreres** (mass grave next to Santa Maria del Mar) and the Rafael Casanova monument.
- **1833–early 1900s** — ***Renaixença***. Catalan literary and cultural revival.
- **~1880–1920** — ***Modernisme***. Gaudí (Sagrada Família begun 1882, Park Güell, Casa Batlló, Casa Milà), Domènech i Montaner (Palau de la Música, Hospital Sant Pau), Puig i Cadafalch.
- **1931–39** — **Second Spanish Republic**; Generalitat restored.
- **1936–39** — **Spanish Civil War**. Barcelona is a Republican stronghold; falls to Franco in January 1939.
- **1939–75** — **Franco dictatorship**. Catalan banned in public, education, signage. Cultural life goes underground.
- **1975–78** — Transition to democracy. Catalan legalized.
- **1978** — Spanish Constitution; Catalonia becomes an autonomous community.
- **1992** — Barcelona Olympics. Major urban transformation: opening the seafront, the Olympic Port, Montjuïc redevelopment.
- **2006–10** — New Statute of Autonomy passed, then partly struck down by the Constitutional Court.
- **2017** — Independence referendum (1 October) and declaration (27 October). See `catalan-identity.md`.
- **2024** — Amnesty law passed for those prosecuted over 2017 events.

## Places where this history is visible

- **Tarraco / Tarragona** — Roman ruins.
- **Born neighborhood / El Born CCM** — preserved 1714 city ruins under a market hall.
- **Fossar de les Moreres** — eternal flame for the 1714 dead.
- **MUHBA (Barcelona History Museum)** at Plaça del Rei — Roman city visible underground.
- **MNAC** (Montjuïc) — world-class Romanesque and Catalan Modernisme collections.
- **Museu d'Història de Catalunya** (Palau de Mar) — pan-Catalan history, good for orientation.
