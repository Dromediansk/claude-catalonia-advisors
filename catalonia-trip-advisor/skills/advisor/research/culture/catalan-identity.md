# Catalan Identity

Catalonia (*Catalunya*) is an autonomous community of Spain with its own language, institutions, traditions, and a distinct national identity. Many Catalans consider themselves a separate nation; treating "Spain" and "Catalonia" as interchangeable can come across as dismissive.

## Key things visitors should know

- Catalonia has its own parliament (*Parlament de Catalunya*), regional government (*Generalitat*), and police force (*Mossos d'Esquadra*) — separate from Spain's national police.
- Catalan (*català*) is co-official with Spanish (*castellà*) and is the default language of regional institutions, schools, and most public signage.
- Cultural symbols (flag, anthem *Els Segadors*, national day, dances like the *sardana*, human towers / *castells*) are markers of identity, not just folklore.
- Avoid statements like "you're Spanish, right?" — many will politely correct you. Equally, avoid assuming everyone wants independence. Opinions split roughly down the middle.

## The two flags

- ***Senyera*** — four red stripes on yellow. The official flag of Catalonia. Hung from many balconies, used at all official events. Non-political: flying it just means "Catalan".
- ***Estelada*** — the *senyera* with an added triangle and star. Symbol of the **independence movement**.
  - ***Estelada blava*** (blue triangle, white star) — traditional / left-leaning independentist.
  - ***Estelada vermella*** (red triangle, yellow star) — socialist/leftist independentist variant.
- Visitors should not buy or wear an *estelada* casually — it is a political statement, not a souvenir.

## Diada Nacional de Catalunya (11 September)

- Catalonia's national day. Commemorates the **fall of Barcelona in 1714** during the War of the Spanish Succession, when Catalan institutions were abolished.
- Expect mass demonstrations, *senyeres* and *esteladas* on balconies, official ceremonies at the Fossar de les Moreres and the monument to Rafael Casanova.
- Many shops close or open short hours. Public transport runs but central streets are closed for marches.
- Tone is mostly festive but politically charged — visitors are welcome to watch; avoid wearing Spanish national symbols that day.

## 2017 referendum and political status (neutral summary)

- On **1 October 2017** the Catalan regional government held an independence referendum that the Spanish Constitutional Court had declared unconstitutional. Turnout was around 43%; of those who voted, roughly 90% chose "yes". Opponents largely boycotted.
- The Catalan Parliament passed a **declaration of independence on 27 October 2017**. It received no international recognition and produced no legal effect.
- The Spanish government invoked Article 155 of the Constitution, dissolved the Catalan Parliament, and called new elections. Several Catalan leaders were prosecuted; others went into exile.
- In **2021** the Spanish government pardoned imprisoned leaders. In **2024** an amnesty law was passed covering those prosecuted for events around the referendum.
- As of 2026 Catalonia remains an autonomous community of Spain. Support for independence has cooled somewhat from its 2017 peak but the issue remains live in regional politics. [verify current poll numbers if asked]

## Practical guidance for visitors

- Don't take sides in conversation. "It's complicated" is a perfectly acceptable answer.
- Learn and use the words *Catalunya* and *català* — pronouncing them right earns goodwill.
- Compliments about Catalan culture, language, and food land well. Comparisons to Madrid generally do not.
- Bullfighting was banned in Catalonia in 2010 — do not ask where to see one.
