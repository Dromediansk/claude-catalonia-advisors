# Food

Catalan cuisine is its own thing. Paella is Valencian, sangria is mostly a tourist drink, and flamenco is Andalusian — none of the three are Catalan traditions. Eat accordingly.

## Must-try Catalan dishes

- ***Pa amb tomàquet*** (bread with tomato) — sliced rustic bread rubbed with ripe tomato, olive oil, salt. The Catalan base dish; served with almost everything.
- ***Escalivada*** — roasted aubergine, red pepper, onion. Often served cold with anchovies or on toast.
- ***Esqueixada*** — shredded raw salt cod (*bacallà*) with tomato, onion, olives, vinegar. Summer dish.
- ***Calçots*** with ***romesco*** — long spring onions charred on the grill, dipped in romesco (almond-pepper-tomato sauce). **Season: roughly January to March only**. *Calçotades* are messy outdoor feasts; bib provided.
- ***Fideuà*** — like paella but made with short noodles instead of rice, usually seafood. Served with *allioli* (garlic mayo).
- ***Arròs negre*** — black rice cooked in squid ink. Often confused with paella; it's not.
- ***Suquet de peix*** — fishermen's fish-and-potato stew.
- ***Botifarra amb mongetes*** — grilled Catalan sausage with white beans. Workingman's classic.
- ***Fricandó*** — slow-braised beef with wild mushrooms (*moixernons*).
- ***Canelons*** — Catalan cannelloni, traditionally eaten on **Sant Esteve** (26 December) using Christmas leftovers.
- ***Bombes*** — fried potato balls stuffed with spiced meat, served with *allioli* and spicy sauce. **Barceloneta specialty** (La Cova Fumada is the original).
- ***Esgarraet***-style anchovies, ***anxoves de l'Escala*** — top-tier cured anchovies; worth ordering on toast.
- ***Crema catalana*** — citrus-and-cinnamon custard with a torched sugar crust. The Catalan crème brûlée (and arguably the original).
- ***Mel i mató*** — fresh unsalted cheese with honey and walnuts.
- ***Xuixo*** — Girona-origin cream-filled fried pastry.

## Tapas, *tapes*, and *pintxos*

- ***Tapes*** (Catalan) / ***tapas*** (Spanish) — small plates. Order several to share.
- ***Pintxos*** — Basque-style snacks on bread, usually held together by a toothpick (count the picks to pay). Common in dedicated *pintxos* bars across Barcelona despite being a Basque thing.
- ***Montaditos*** — small open-faced sandwiches.
- ***Ració*** / ***ración*** — a larger sharing plate (bigger than a tapa).

## Drinks worth knowing

- ***Vermut*** (vermouth) — red, herbal, on the rocks with an olive and an orange slice. **Vermut hour** is roughly **Sunday 12:00–14:00** — Catalans gather pre-lunch for one. *"Fer el vermut"* = to do the vermouth ritual.
- ***Cava*** — Catalan sparkling wine made by the traditional method, mostly from the Penedès region. Cheaper and often as good as Champagne.
- ***Estrella Damm*** — the local Barcelona beer. *Una clara* = beer + lemon soda (shandy).
- ***Cremat*** — flamed rum coffee, often served with *havaneres* singing.
- Wine: ask for ***vi de la casa*** (house wine). Local DOs include Penedès, Priorat (big reds), Empordà, Montsant.
- **Sangria warning**: rarely drunk by locals. If you must, ***clara*** or ***tinto de verano*** (red wine + lemon soda) is what locals actually drink in summer.

## How meals work

- **Breakfast** (*esmorzar*) — small: coffee + pastry, or a *bikini* (ham-and-cheese toastie).
- **Mid-morning** — second breakfast around 10–11 (*entrepà*, beer or coffee).
- **Lunch** (*dinar*) — the big meal, **roughly 14:00–16:00**. *Menú del dia* is served at this time only.
- **Merienda / berenar** — afternoon snack ~17:00–18:00.
- **Dinner** (*sopar*) — late, **typically 21:00–23:00**. Kitchens at non-tourist places often don't open before 20:30.

## ***Menú del dia***

- **Fixed-price lunch menu, Monday–Friday**, roughly €12–18 in neighborhood spots, €18–25 in nicer ones. [verify current price range]
- Usually: starter + main + dessert or coffee + bread + drink (water, wine, or beer included).
- Best value in Spanish food. Look for handwritten chalkboards outside.

## Markets

- ***La Boqueria*** (Mercat de Sant Josep, on La Rambla) — gorgeous, historic, **very touristy**. Go **early** (before 10:00) before tour groups arrive, eat at a counter (Pinotxo, El Quim de la Boqueria), avoid the fruit-cup vendors near the entrance.
- ***Mercat de Sant Antoni*** (Eixample) — restored 19th-c. market, more local feel, good food bars. Closed Sundays except for the book market outside.
- ***Mercat de Santa Caterina*** (Born) — colorful wavy roof by Miralles, locals' market, excellent eateries.
- ***Mercat de la Llibertat*** (Gràcia) — small, neighborhood, almost no tourists.
- ***Mercat de la Concepció*** (Eixample) — daily flower market open 24/7 on one side.

## Where to AVOID

- **Anywhere on Las Ramblas with photo menus or "tourist menu" boards** — overpriced, microwaved, generally bad.
- **Restaurants with hosts on the street pulling you in** — same.
- **Anywhere advertising "paella + sangria" combos** as the main draw, especially in the Gothic Quarter and along the beachfront.
- **Beach-front "chiringuito" paella** unless someone you trust recommends the specific spot.
- The fruit-juice cups at the Boqueria entrance are often days old; buy fruit from the back instead.

## Reservation tips

- **Book ahead** for any well-known restaurant — same day at lunch, days ahead at dinner, weeks ahead for places with stars.
- Walk-ins are easier at neighborhood bars and *menú del dia* spots.
- Tipping: not expected. Rounding up or leaving 5–10% for good service is generous.
